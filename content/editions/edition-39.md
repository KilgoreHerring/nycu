## Opening

The agent question stopped being theoretical this fortnight. Harvey revealed Spectre, an autonomous company agent that monitors operations and acts without human prompts. Its research team published harness engineering results showing agents teaching themselves to do legal work at 87.7% accuracy - up from 40.8%. A Finnish lawyer built a 66-person AI law firm on a Mac Mini and gave it 30 days to find a commercial home before open-sourcing it. And while the frontier pushes forward, courts handed out $145K in AI hallucination sanctions in Q1 alone - on track to triple last year's volume - and a gaming CEO's deleted ChatGPT logs just became the star witness in a $250 million case.

The pattern is becoming familiar: the capability is real, the pace is accelerating, and the consequences of getting the human side wrong are getting more expensive. But this fortnight added a new dimension. Harvey is not just building tools for lawyers. It is building agents that improve themselves. That is a different category of product, and it demands a different kind of attention from anyone responsible for AI governance, adoption, or strategy.

### Harvey's Spectre Agent and the Self-Improving Legal AI
[Artificial Lawyer - Spectre](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/04/03/harveys-spectre-agent-points-to-law-firm-world-model/) | [Artificial Lawyer - Harness Engineering](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/04/07/harvey-drives-legal-agent-learning-via-harness-engineering/)

Two Harvey announcements in the same week that, taken together, represent the most significant product development in legal AI this quarter.

- **What:** Harvey unveiled Spectre, an autonomous company agent that operates independently inside the business. According to Harvey President Gabe Pereyra, "much of what Spectre does is no longer triggered by a human prompt. It is triggered by the system monitoring the company and making decisions based on incidents, bug reports, customer feedback, and Slack messages." Spectre represents what Pereyra calls a "law firm world model" - a live picture of what is happening inside an organisation and what needs to happen next. Separately, Harvey published research on "harness engineering" - a method where agents teach themselves to do legal work through iterative self-improvement. The experiment ran across 12 internal benchmark tasks spanning lease reviews, complaint drafting, tax memos, disclosure schedules, and due diligence. A coding agent read evaluation feedback, clustered failures, hypothesised improvements, built or edited components, and re-ran tasks. Average scores jumped from 40.8% to 87.7%. Seven of twelve tasks exceeded 90%. One hit 100%. The agents developed their own legal-specific capabilities: cross-document review playbooks, validation checkpoints, structured fact sheets, and automated output formatting - none of which were pre-programmed.

- **So what:** The harness engineering results deserve close attention from anyone responsible for AI strategy. The core finding is that given examples of good work and a clear rubric, agents can auto-generate their own toolkits and meaningfully improve their own performance. That is not a chatbot getting marginally better at answering questions. That is a system learning how to do legal work by studying what good legal work looks like. The practical implication for law firms is significant: Harvey is building agents that get better at a firm's specific work the more they do it, without needing model updates or manual prompt engineering. Spectre takes this further by removing the human trigger entirely. Pereyra's observation that "more work can happen than the old coordination structure can absorb" reframes the bottleneck. The constraint is no longer capacity to do the work. It is capacity to review, prioritise, and coordinate the work being done. For partners and senior lawyers, that is a fundamental shift in what the job requires. Every lawyer, as Pereyra puts it, "is now prized for their judgment, not their output." The firms that understand what that means - for staffing, pricing, apprenticeship, and client relationships - are the ones building a durable advantage. The ones still thinking about AI as a faster drafting tool are already behind.

### A Gaming CEO Used ChatGPT to Dodge $250M. The Court Found Everything.
[Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/17/krafton-subnautica-chatgpt-delaware-court-ruling-ceo-reinstated/) | [Spellbook](https://www.spellbook.legal/newsletter/krafton-ceos-deleted-chatgpt-logs-become-star-witness-in-250m-subnautica-2-case)

A Delaware court ruling that should be required reading for every governance committee and in-house legal team.

- **What:** Changhan Kim, CEO of South Korean gaming giant Krafton, acquired game studio Unknown Worlds Entertainment for $500 million in 2021. The deal included a $250 million earnout bonus if the sequel to Subnautica hit specific sales targets. Internal projections showed the bonus would be triggered. Rather than consult his legal team, Kim turned to ChatGPT. When the chatbot initially said the earnout would be "difficult to cancel," Kim persisted with follow-up prompts until it produced what became known internally as "Project X" - a detailed corporate takeover strategy. The AI suggested forming a task force to renegotiate or force a takeover, controlling publishing rights and game code, and framing the conflict around "fan trust" rather than finances. Kim even asked ChatGPT to draft public messaging. He then had the studio's CEO and cofounders removed without legitimate cause. Kim admitted at trial that he had deleted specific ChatGPT conversation logs. Delaware's Court of Chancery Vice Chancellor Lori Will ruled against Krafton, finding that executives are "expected to exercise independent human judgment - not outsource good-faith decisions to an AI." The court ordered reinstatement of the removed executives and extended the earnout period to account for the disruption.

- **So what:** Three lessons from this case matter for anyone advising on AI governance. First, AI chat logs are discoverable. They are subpoenable. Deleting them does not make them disappear - it makes them look worse. Any organisation using AI tools for strategic decision-making needs to understand that these conversations are part of the evidentiary record, just like emails and text messages. Second, the judge's finding on independent human judgment sets a marker. Using AI to generate a strategy is one thing. Following AI-generated advice to circumvent a contractual obligation without applying independent judgment is a governance failure that courts will scrutinise. Third, the accountability point is stark. ChatGPT did not breach a fiduciary duty. Kim did. The AI was the instrument, not the decision-maker, and the law does not recognise "the chatbot told me to" as a defence. For governance committees reviewing AI acceptable use policies, this case is the clearest illustration yet of why guardrails on AI-assisted strategic decision-making are not theoretical. The Krafton ruling demonstrates real consequences, in a real court, for real money.

### $145K in Q1: AI Hallucination Sanctions Hit Record Pace
[NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/03/nx-s1-5761454/penalties-stack-up-ai-spreads-through-legal-system) | [Damien Charlotin Database](https://www.damiencharlotin.com/hallucinations/)

NPR's coverage of the sanctions wave brought AI hallucinations in legal filings to a mainstream audience for the first time. The numbers now demand attention.

- **What:** Courts imposed $145,000 in sanctions against lawyers for AI-generated errors in Q1 2026 alone. Damien Charlotin's database, which tracks AI hallucination cases worldwide, has now surpassed 1,200 documented incidents - approximately 800 from US courts. The pace implies an annualised rate approaching 1,400 cases in 2026, nearly triple 2025's volume. Charlotin noted that "recently we had 10 cases from 10 different courts on a single day." The landmark Oregon case saw a federal court order $109,700 in combined sanctions and adverse costs - the largest single penalty to date - after an attorney filed a brief containing fictitious AI-generated case citations without verification. In Nebraska, the high court investigated attorney Greg Lake over a brief containing fabricated citations; Lake denied using AI and claimed a computer malfunction. The Sixth Circuit levied $30,000 against two attorneys for AI-fabricated citations. An Oregon attorney was fined $10,000 - $500 per fabricated citation. NPR's reporting, syndicated across dozens of stations, brought the issue to a general audience that legal trade press had been covering for over two years.

- **So what:** The NPR coverage is a tipping point. When a story moves from Artificial Lawyer and Bloomberg Law to national public radio, it has crossed from industry concern to public awareness. That matters because it changes the conversation with clients, with courts, and with regulators. The enforcement trajectory is unambiguous. Sanctions are getting larger - from hundreds of dollars in 2023 to six figures in 2026. Courts are getting less patient. And the defences are getting weaker - "I didn't know it hallucinated" is no longer novel; it is negligent. For any firm that has not yet implemented mandatory verification protocols for AI-assisted research, the risk calculus has shifted. The question is no longer whether sanctions will follow unverified AI output. It is how large the sanctions will be. The French National Bar Association has already adopted ethics guidance confirming disciplinary proceedings. The UK's CJC consultation on enhanced AI statements of truth closes on 14 April. The regulatory net is tightening across jurisdictions. The firms that treated AI verification as a nice-to-have are running out of time to make it mandatory.

### Altman's New Deal: From Cash Transfers to Robot Taxes
[OpenAI - Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age/) | [OpenResearch - Unconditional Cash Study](https://www.openresearchlab.org/findings)

Sam Altman published a 13-page policy blueprint that deserves attention beyond the headlines - both for what it says and what it reveals about the trajectory of thinking at the company building the technology most likely to reshape professional work.

- **What:** OpenAI released "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age: Ideas to Keep People First" on 6 April. Altman frames it as the beginning of a new social contract on the scale of the Progressive Era and the New Deal. Six headline proposals: a Public Wealth Fund seeded by AI companies (modelled on Alaska's Permanent Fund) that distributes returns directly to citizens; robot and automation taxes to replace eroding payroll tax revenue; 32-hour workweek pilots with no pay cut, framed as an "efficiency dividend"; automatic safety net triggers that activate when AI displacement metrics hit preset thresholds; expanded investment in human-centred job sectors (care, education, healthcare); and containment playbooks for autonomous AI systems that "cannot be easily recalled." The paper also proposes treating AI access as "foundational" - like literacy or electricity - and calls for portable benefits decoupled from individual employers. On the safety side, proposals include incident reporting systems, international coordination networks modelled on multilateral institutions, and a requirement that frontier AI companies adopt Public Benefit Corporation governance. OpenAI is backing the conversation with research grants of up to $100,000 plus $1 million in API credits.

  The paper sits on top of OpenResearch's Unconditional Cash Study - the largest UBI experiment conducted in the US, personally funded by Altman. Three thousand participants across Texas and Illinois received $1,000 per month for three years. The results, published from mid-2024 onwards, were genuinely mixed. Recipients worked 1.3 fewer hours per week and were 2 percentage points less likely to be employed. But they were 10% more likely to be job searching and significantly more selective about roles - prioritising meaningful work over any work. They were 14% more likely to pursue education or training. Black recipients were 26% more likely to start a business. Spending went to basics - food, rent, transport - not discretionary consumption. There were health improvements (20% less problematic drinking, 10% more dental care) alongside puzzling negatives (26% more hospitalisations). And the headline that matters most politically: recipients valued work more, not less. The "people will just stop working" argument took a direct empirical hit.

  But the study also showed that $1,000 a month does not transform life trajectories. Children's academic and behavioural outcomes did not measurably improve. The structural barriers - housing costs, healthcare access, education quality - remained. Cash bought time and agency. It did not buy systemic change.

- **So what:** The evolution in Altman's thinking is the story here. In 2021, his position was straightforward: AI will create abundance, we should give people money. By 2024, his own research showed that giving people money produces modest, mixed results - helpful at the margins but not transformative. By April 2026, the prescription has shifted to structural reform: ownership stakes rather than transfers, tax base restructuring rather than redistribution, institutional change rather than individual payments.

  That intellectual journey is worth tracking because it maps onto a broader shift in how AI companies talk about economic disruption. The early narrative was technology-first: build the thing, the abundance will follow, hand out cash to cover the transition. The current narrative is institutions-first: the technology will arrive regardless, but without new institutions - wealth funds, portable benefits, automatic stabilisers - the gains will concentrate and the disruption will be unevenly absorbed.

  For legal services specifically, three proposals have direct relevance. First, the efficiency dividends concept - productivity gains from AI converted into shorter working weeks, better benefits, and time back for workers - is exactly the conversation law firms should be having instead of the one they are having (how to maintain billable hours while using AI to do the work faster). Second, portable benefits decoupled from employers matter in a profession where career paths increasingly involve moves between firms, in-house roles, ALSPs, and consulting. Third, the "Right to AI" framing - treating access as foundational infrastructure rather than competitive advantage - challenges the firms hoarding AI capabilities as differentiators.

  The self-serving elements are worth naming. OpenAI is essentially writing the regulatory framework it wants to operate under. The auditing regime proposal conveniently limits heavy controls to "a small number of companies and the most advanced models" while preserving the broader ecosystem - which happens to be the competitive landscape where OpenAI sits at the top. The Public Benefit Corporation call landed the same week OpenAI completed its own corporate restructuring. And the research grants ($100K cash, $1M API credits) are a modest price for positioning OpenAI as the convener of the policy conversation rather than the subject of it.

  But the substance stands independently of the motive. The adaptive safety nets with automatic triggers are genuinely interesting policy design. The Public Wealth Fund is a more sophisticated mechanism than UBI. And the efficiency dividends framing - treating shorter working weeks as a return on productivity investment rather than a concession - is politically astute in a way that matters for anyone trying to sell workforce transformation to a partnership or a board.

### Anthropic's Mythos Finds Thousands of Zero-Days. Anthropic Locks It in a Box.
[TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/07/anthropic-mythos-ai-model-preview-security/) | [Anthropic - Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing) | [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/07/anthropic-claude-mythos-ai-hackers-cyberattacks.html)

Anthropic did something this fortnight that no AI company has done before: built a model so dangerous it decided not to release it.

- **What:** Claude Mythos Preview is a new frontier model with cybersecurity capabilities that surprised even Anthropic's own researchers. During internal testing, Mythos autonomously discovered thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities across every major operating system and browser - including a 17-year-old remote code execution flaw in FreeBSD that had evaded the entire security industry. Rather than releasing Mythos commercially, Anthropic launched Project Glasswing: a restricted programme limiting access to approximately 50 partner organisations including Amazon, Apple, Microsoft, CrowdStrike, and the Linux Foundation. Anthropic is committing $100M in usage credits and $4M to open-source security organisations. The model scores 93.9% on SWE-bench Verified - higher than any publicly available model - but it is not being sold. It is being deployed as defensive infrastructure. Simon Willison, one of the most respected voices in the AI developer community, commented simply that the restricted release "sounds necessary to me."

- **So what:** There are two stories here, and the second one is more important than the first. The first story is capability: an AI model that can find vulnerabilities humans missed for nearly two decades is a genuinely new category of tool. The offensive implications are obvious and alarming. The defensive implications are potentially transformative for every organisation running critical infrastructure.

  The second story is governance. Anthropic had a model that would generate enormous commercial interest and chose not to sell it. In a market where every competitor is racing to ship, that is a striking decision. Whether you read it as genuine responsibility, strategic positioning, or both, it sets a precedent. The question for every other frontier lab is now explicit: if your model discovers something dangerous, what do you do with it?

  For law firms and in-house teams, the practical takeaway is about cybersecurity posture. The Jones Day breach this same week (Silent Ransom Group, Accellion platform, 10 clients' data, $13M ransom demand) is a reminder that legal services remain a high-value target. If AI can find vulnerabilities that human security teams missed for 17 years, the attack surface just changed. The firms and departments that invest in AI-augmented security testing now are buying time. The ones that wait are betting that attackers will not get access to similar capabilities. That is not a bet worth making.

### Microsoft Copilot Goes Multi-Model. The Single-Vendor Era is Over.
[GeekWire](https://www.geekwire.com/2026/microsoft-365-copilot-and-the-end-of-the-single-model-era-in-enterprise-ai/) | [Microsoft Blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-365/blog/2026/03/30/copilot-cowork-now-available-in-frontier/)

Microsoft just made the most important architectural decision in enterprise AI this year. Most people will miss why.

- **What:** Microsoft 365 Copilot's Researcher agent can now use OpenAI's GPT to draft a response, then have Anthropic's Claude review it for accuracy, completeness, and citation quality before finalising. Two new features make this work: Critique (one model checks another's output) and Council (multiple models collaborate on complex tasks). The result is a 13.8% improvement on Deep Research benchmarks. This ships as part of Microsoft 365 E7 "Frontier Suite" from 1 May at $99/user/month, bundling E5, Copilot, Entra Suite, and Agent 365 - a new control plane for governing and scaling agents across the enterprise. Agent 365 includes full A2A (agent-to-agent) support, meaning agents built in Copilot Studio can communicate with and delegate to third-party agents using an open protocol. Separately, Copilot Cowork - long-running, multi-step agentic work powered by Anthropic's models - is now available to Frontier subscribers with enterprise-grade controls.

- **So what:** The single-model era lasted about three years and it is already over. Microsoft - OpenAI's largest investor - is now routing work to Claude because it produces better results for certain tasks. That tells you everything you need to know about where enterprise AI is heading.

  For law firms and in-house teams, there are three immediate implications. First, the "which AI provider should we bet on" question has been answered: you should not bet on one. The platform layer (Microsoft, in most cases) will abstract the model layer, routing work to whichever model performs best for a given task. Your AI strategy should be provider-agnostic, focused on workflow design and data governance rather than model selection.

  Second, the Critique pattern - one model checking another's work - is directly relevant to the hallucination problem. If Microsoft is using Claude to verify GPT's citations, that is an architectural acknowledgement that no single model is reliable enough on its own. Law firms building AI workflows should be thinking about verification as a design principle, not an afterthought.

  Third, the A2A protocol matters more than it sounds. When agents can delegate to other agents across organisational boundaries, the integration question changes. Today, connecting Harvey to iManage to your document management system is a custom engineering project. In a world where agents communicate through an open protocol, it becomes configuration. The firms investing in agent orchestration infrastructure now are the ones that will move fastest when these protocols mature.

### Anthropic Goes Direct: Claude for Word Lands on the Lawyer's Desktop
[Artificial Lawyer](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/04/11/anthropic-targets-lawyers-with-claude-for-word/)

Three days after Microsoft shipped multi-model Copilot with Claude as its accuracy checker, Anthropic shipped something that changes the deal.

- **What:** Anthropic launched Claude for Word in beta on 11 April, explicitly positioning legal contract review as the flagship use case. The add-in gives Claude read and write access to the document, with demo use cases that are unusually specific for an Anthropic launch: summarising commercial terms, flagging non-standard provisions, mutualising indemnities, and working through tracked-changes comment threads without breaking track changes. Availability is limited to Claude Team and Enterprise plans, which puts it on a direct commercial footing with Harvey and Legora for the first time. Distribution is through Anthropic's own admin console rather than the Office marketplace.

- **So what:** The Microsoft multi-model story and the Claude for Word story are two halves of the same movement and they point in opposite directions. Microsoft's bet is that the platform layer abstracts the model layer - agents route work to whichever frontier lab performs best, and the customer never has to care which one. Anthropic's bet is that frontier labs do not have to accept being routed to. They can ship directly into the surface where the work happens and build their own commercial relationship with the lawyer doing the drafting. Both bets can be right at once, but they imply very different market structures three years out.

  For law firms, there are two immediate implications. First, the legal AI vendor moat just got narrower. Harvey and Legora have spent billions building platforms whose core advantage, for many users, is that they meet lawyers inside a document. Claude for Word meets them in the same place, with a frontier model behind it, for the price of a Claude Enterprise seat. The differentiation now has to come from legal-specific workflows, proprietary data, deep integrations, and workflow orchestration - not from "AI in Word." The vendors that have invested in those deeper layers will be fine. The ones selling a thin wrapper on a frontier model will feel this first.

  Second, the procurement question just got messier. A firm running Microsoft 365 Copilot with multi-model routing, Harvey, Legora, and now Claude for Word is paying four separate vendors for overlapping capabilities inside the same document. That is not a sustainable steady state. Either the platforms consolidate, the frontier labs win the direct relationship, or firms get aggressive about standardising. The procurement and governance teams that have been putting off the "which AI stack are we building on" conversation are about to find it forced on them.

  The third-order effect is the one worth watching. If Anthropic can ship Claude for Word, OpenAI can ship GPT for Word, and Google already has Gemini in Docs. The next twelve months will test whether the platform-as-orchestrator model Microsoft is betting on survives contact with frontier labs that have no intention of being commoditised. The firms that are provider-agnostic at the workflow level - designing their AI operations around tasks rather than around a particular vendor - will move more confidently through this than the ones that picked a single horse last year.

### The UK AI Paradox: Sovereign Fund Launches as OpenAI Pulls Investment
[GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/uk-will-win-ai-race-as-chancellor-sets-out-economic-big-choices) | [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/openai-pauses-stargate-uk-data-center-effort-citing-energy-costs) | [New Statesman](https://www.newstatesman.com/technology/2026/04/the-silent-coup)

Two stories landed in the same week that, taken together, capture the contradictions at the heart of UK AI policy.

- **What:** On one side: the Chancellor's $500M Sovereign AI Fund formally launches on 16 April at Wayve's offices, with James Wise from Balderton Capital as inaugural chair. It is part of a broader $2.5bn government commitment to AI and quantum computing, with the stated ambition of making the UK the "fastest AI adopter in the G7." The fund will give British companies access to capital, compute, and support.

  On the other side: OpenAI paused its UK data centre project - the Stargate initiative launched alongside Trump's visit in September 2025 - citing unfavourable regulatory conditions and high energy costs. The key concern, per Bloomberg's reporting, is uncertainty over whether the UK will change copyright law to allow AI training on copyrighted works. The government has backed away from a proposed broad text-and-data-mining exception, adopting what the Library of Congress characterised as a "wait and see" approach. The AI Bill itself has been delayed until after the next King's Speech, expected May 2026 at the earliest.

  Meanwhile, a New Statesman investigation revealed that AI-generated text has already been incorporated into an Act of Parliament. Government officials confirmed this was achieved before the UAE's publicly announced AI-written laws. The investigation found AI operating throughout government - drafting emails, summarising briefings, composing speeches, and influencing the 2025 Spending Review (which mentioned AI 38 times). Sixty House of Lords members have declarable AI interests. The revolving door between tech companies and government is accelerating.

- **So what:** The UK is simultaneously trying to attract AI investment, repel AI investment, and quietly integrate AI into its own operations. That is not a strategy. That is three strategies colliding.

  The Sovereign AI Fund is a sensible intervention - patient capital for British AI companies that might otherwise sell early to US acquirers or relocate. But $500M is a rounding error against the billions being deployed by OpenAI, Anthropic, and the hyperscalers. It signals intent without matching the scale of the ambition.

  OpenAI's Stargate pause is more damaging than the headlines suggest. It is not just one data centre. It is a signal to every AI company evaluating the UK as a location for compute infrastructure. The copyright impasse is the stated reason, but the underlying problem is regulatory uncertainty. Companies building billion-dollar infrastructure need predictable policy frameworks. "Wait and see" is not a framework. It is an invitation to build somewhere else.

  The New Statesman piece adds a layer of irony. The government is using AI extensively in its own operations while failing to create the regulatory conditions for the private sector to do the same. AI is already in legislation, in policy development, in the machinery of government. The question of whether AI should be embedded in professional work has been answered, quietly, by the institution that has not yet decided how to regulate it.

  For law firms advising on UK AI strategy, the practical message is: plan for regulatory ambiguity to continue through 2026. The AI Bill will arrive eventually, but its scope and timing remain uncertain. Copyright policy is unresolved. Energy costs are structural. The firms that help clients build AI capabilities despite this uncertainty - rather than waiting for clarity that may not come - are the ones adding value right now.
